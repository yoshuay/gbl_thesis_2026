# Deep Literature Integration: Core Thesis Pillars

This memo extends the deeper literature pass beyond the gamification cluster. The purpose is to identify how the main DGBL, LM-GM, CLT, card-game, adult-learning, and telemetry sources improve the thesis claims.

## 1. All et al. (2021): Absolute effectiveness is different from relative effectiveness

All et al. is useful for more than the generic "DGBL studies need rigor" claim. Their feasibility work distinguishes absolute effectiveness from relative effectiveness. A game can improve learning compared with no added instruction, while still failing to add value compared with a simpler digital alternative.

Use in thesis:

- Strengthens the need for a content-matched control.
- Supports the interpretation that a game needs to justify its additional development and completion cost.
- Gives a direct precedent for corporate/professional DGBL not automatically outperforming simpler technology-delivered instruction.

Best wording direction:

The study does not ask whether the sales game is better than nothing. It asks whether the mechanics layer adds value beyond a meaningful conventional digital module.

## 2. Tay et al. (2022): Professional upskilling requires selective game design

Tay et al. is stronger when used as an adult/professional-learning source rather than a general DGBL review. The review states that adult professional upskilling is underexamined, that adult learners bring varied knowledge and experience, and that not all game elements are necessary or beneficial.

Use in thesis:

- Supports the sales-training context as a professional-upskilling problem.
- Justifies focusing on learner readiness.
- Supports the claim that game elements should be pedagogically selected, not added as engagement garnish.

Best wording direction:

Professional learners do not enter as blank-slate students; their prior schema, work expectations, and time tolerance shape whether a game feels useful or costly.

## 3. Arnab et al. (2015): LM-GM is a mapping lens, not an effectiveness guarantee

Arnab et al. is central, but the most useful part is the limitation. LM-GM maps mechanics to pedagogical intent and helps reveal when gameplay and pedagogy intertwine. It also remains descriptive rather than prescriptive and does not yet include a player model.

Use in thesis:

- Defends LM-GM as the design framework.
- Creates space for the Cognitive Budget Framework: alignment needs a readiness/cost check.
- Prevents overclaiming that LM-GM-designed games should automatically reduce cognitive load.

Best wording direction:

LM-GM answers "what learning function is this mechanic meant to serve?" The Cognitive Budget Framework adds "can this learner afford to operate the mechanic yet?"

## 4. Plass et al. (2015): Engagement has to return to cognitive engagement

Plass et al. should anchor more than the four pillars. Their key move is that games involve cognitive, motivational, affective, and sociocultural foundations, and that different types of engagement only matter for learning when they support cognitive engagement with the learning mechanic.

Use in thesis:

- Supports demoting engagement from explanation to mechanism candidate.
- Supports self-efficacy mediation: engagement matters when it becomes productive confidence and adaptive selling belief.
- Supports adaptation: games should measure prior knowledge or self-regulation before adapting.

Best wording direction:

The game may engage learners behaviorally or affectively without engaging them cognitively in adaptive selling.

## 5. Proulx et al. (2017): The same mechanic can carry different motivational meanings

Proulx et al. combine LM-GM with self-determination theory. The strongest point is contextual: the motivational meaning of a mechanic depends on how the mechanic is implemented. Progression, for example, can be controlling or autonomy-supportive.

Use in thesis:

- Strengthens H2 and the mediation argument.
- Explains why self-efficacy and engagement must be measured rather than assumed.
- Supports the idea that LM-GM should be extended with motivational and learner-readiness checks.

Best wording direction:

Mechanics are not motivational by default; their motivational meaning is enacted through implementation and learner interpretation.

## 6. Kalyuga (2007): Cognitive load is learner-relative

Kalyuga is doing more than "novices need support." The important point is that cognitive load depends on the learner's knowledge base. The same material can impose different processing demands because prior schema changes what counts as a single coordinated element.

Use in thesis:

- Supports the expertise/readiness moderation.
- Clarifies the content-matched interpretation: intrinsic content can be held constant by design while experienced load still differs by learner.
- Supports the schema-offset component of the Cognitive Budget Framework.

Best wording direction:

The game and control were content-equivalent at the design level, but not cognitively equivalent for every learner.

## 7. Kordaki and Gousiou (2017): Card games are promising but often under-scaffolded

Kordaki and Gousiou support the card format through matching, grouping, sorting, comparing, decision making, reflection, and constructivist learning. The deeper useful point is their warning that many digital card games lack explicit scaffolding during play.

Use in thesis:

- Supports the sales-game card format.
- Supports the expectation that card games can segment and constrain decisions.
- Adds a caution: card format alone does not guarantee cognitive support.

Best wording direction:

Cards can organize complexity, but card play still needs scaffolding if learners are to stay cognitively engaged with the domain.

## 8. Chang and Yang (2023): Scaffolding effects depend on adult learner characteristics

Chang and Yang found that hard and soft scaffolding interacted with adult learners' cognitive style in predicting emotion, cognitive load, and performance. The thesis should not import their cognitive-style taxonomy wholesale, but the conditional-support pattern is directly useful.

Use in thesis:

- Supports the adult-readiness argument.
- Supports the idea that scaffolding is not universally beneficial.
- Strengthens future design: onboarding and adaptive support should vary by learner.

Best wording direction:

The question is not whether scaffolding is present, but whether the type of support fits the learner.

## 9. Sevcenko et al. (2021): Telemetry can estimate cognitive load, but only with theory

Sevcenko et al. show that theory-grounded in-game metrics can be associated with subjective workload and performance, and can support future adaptive systems. Their caution is equally important: behavioral metrics may also reflect stress, emotion, motor processes, or control familiarity.

Use in thesis:

- Supports behavioral logs as triangulating evidence.
- Prevents overclaiming response time as cognitive load.
- Supports future work on real-time adaptation and dynamic difficulty.

Best wording direction:

Telemetry is valuable because it makes learner friction visible, but it becomes psychological evidence only when interpreted alongside theory and psychometrics.

## 10. Manuscript-level effect

The deeper pass improves the thesis in four ways:

- The control condition now matters more: the study tests relative effectiveness, not only whether a game can teach.
- The Cognitive Budget Framework is better anchored: it extends an identified gap in LM-GM rather than appearing as a new label.
- The readiness claim is better protected: learner-relative cognitive load and adult scaffolding interaction support it.
- The telemetry claim is more credible: logs corroborate psychological mechanisms without pretending to replace validated measures.
