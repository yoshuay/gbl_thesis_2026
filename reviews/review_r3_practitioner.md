### Summary Assessment
This paper presents an ambitious and well-structured evaluation of a game-based training intervention for B2B sales, with genuine methodological rigour and honest limitation reporting. From a sales training and HRD perspective, however, the paper's practical contributions are built on foundations that do not fully hold up to scrutiny: the sample is predominantly from the education sector with voluntary enrolment in what appears to be a non-mandatory professional development context, the primary outcomes are self-report belief measures rather than demonstrated behavioural competencies, and the practical recommendation (expertise routing using a pre-ADAPTS screening threshold) makes assumptions about organisational deployment contexts that do not apply to most real-world B2B sales training environments. The format–measurement alignment concern — that the text-based control condition and the ADAPTS-SV text-propositional questionnaire share a surface format advantage — is the most serious threat to the validity of the main comparison, and the paper's treatment of it as a named limitation may be insufficient given how central the ADAPTS null finding is to the study's story. I recommend Major Revision focused on recalibrating the practical implications section and strengthening the outcome validity discussion.

## Strengths

### S1: The Differential Attrition Discussion Is a Genuine Contribution to Training Deployment Thinking
The paper's decision to keep non-completers visible rather than silently excluding them (§6.6) is methodologically commendable and practically important. The observation that a format that loses 41.8% of assigned participants before analysis — disproportionately novices — is a real-world deployment problem irrespective of completer outcomes is well-argued and practically resonant. In organisational training contexts, completion rates are a business metric; a training format with this attrition profile would face serious administrative resistance. The paper's framing of attrition as "part of the substantive evaluation" (p. lvi) is exactly right.

### S2: The Practical Recommendation Is Operationally Specific (Even If Overconfident)
The expertise-routing recommendation (§6.7) is more operationally specific than typical academic training research: it identifies a specific score threshold (≤46.7% ADAPTS), a specific instrument already used in the study (ADAPTS-SV pre-training), and a specific decision action (route novices to direct instruction first). This specificity is valuable for training designers even if the threshold requires validation. The addition of the behavioral log signal (≤5-minute exits as cognitive friction rather than scheduling conflicts) adds a real-time monitoring dimension that is practitioner-relevant.

### S3: The Measurement Timing Argument Has Broad Implications for Training Evaluation Practice
The discussion in §6.8 (Limitation 7) of why immediate post-test comparisons are structurally conservative for game-based conditions — because games produce motivational and schema-based mechanisms that compound post-training rather than expressing immediately — is an important argument that should be read widely in the corporate training research community. The Wouters et al. (2013) citation (retention d=0.36 vs. immediate learning d=0.29) provides empirical grounding. This argument, if taken seriously, would change how training evaluation studies are designed: delayed measurement with on-the-job transfer indicators would become primary rather than supplementary endpoints.

## Weaknesses

### W1: The Sample Does Not Represent the B2B Sales Training Population
**Problem**: The study recruited participants from "a single professional development context, with approximately 69% working in the education sector and the remainder distributed across industries, predominantly from Southeast Asia, in a voluntary online enrolment setting" (§6.8, Limitation 8). B2B sales training in practice typically involves: commercial salespeople in revenue-generating roles (not educators), mandatory participation (not voluntary enrolment), industry-specific domain content, and organisational accountability for training completion. The paper's title, abstract, and practical recommendations speak to "B2B sales training" as a professional domain without adequate qualification of how closely this study's participants resemble that domain.

**Why it matters**: If the key finding — that novice learners (C1) are severely disadvantaged (d=−1.88) — is driven partly by the expertise distribution particular to this sample (educators who are novice adaptive sellers may have very different background schema patterns than, say, junior commercial salespeople), the expertise routing recommendation cannot transfer directly to B2B sales training contexts without validation. The pre-ADAPTS ≤46.7% threshold, in particular, needs to be interpreted relative to the score distribution in the actual deployment population.

**Suggestion**: Add a dedicated paragraph in §6.7 explicitly stating that the practical recommendations are contingent on population characteristics matching this study's sample, and that the ADAPTS threshold must be re-calibrated for different professional populations before deployment. Reframe the language throughout from "B2B sales training" to "adaptive selling training in professional development contexts" to better match the actual participant pool.

**Severity**: Major

### W2: ADAPTS-SV Measures Belief, Not Competence — The Practical Implication Must Be Caveated Accordingly
**Problem**: ADAPTS-SV assesses adaptive selling *beliefs* — self-reported confidence in one's adaptive selling approach (e.g., "I vary my sales style from situation to situation") on a 7-point agreement scale. The paper's practical framing repeatedly implies that ADAPTS-SV captures "adaptive selling competency" or "adaptive selling skill," but it does not. It captures perceived alignment with adaptive selling principles, which may or may not correlate with actual adaptive selling behaviour in client interactions. The paper explicitly acknowledges (§6.8, Limitation 4) that "all competency-related findings should be interpreted as self-report beliefs about adaptive selling, not demonstrated skill acquisition." However, this caveat appears in the Limitations section only; the Discussion (§6.7) and Conclusion continue to speak of "adaptive selling" improvements in language that implies demonstrated skill.

**Why it matters**: From an HRD perspective, the distinction between beliefs (distal) and behaviour (proximal to performance) is critical for evaluating training effectiveness. Training that changes beliefs may or may not change behaviours; the intention–behaviour gap in training transfer is well-documented (Blume et al., 2010). The paper's finding of no ADAPTS gain at the aggregate level cannot be taken as evidence that the game failed to develop adaptive selling competency — because competency was not measured.

**Suggestion**: Consistently qualify references to "adaptive selling" outcomes throughout §6 and §7 as "self-reported adaptive selling beliefs." When recommending the ADAPTS-SV threshold for expertise routing, note explicitly that this threshold predicts cluster membership defined by belief scores, not demonstrated behavioural outcomes. Add one sentence to the Conclusion noting that future studies should include a behavioural or objective competency measure as a primary outcome.

**Severity**: Major

### W3: Format–Measurement Alignment Is a More Serious Threat Than the Limitations Section Acknowledges
**Problem**: §6.8 (Limitation 6) correctly identifies the format–measurement alignment concern: the control condition delivers content in "propositional text slides" and the ADAPTS-SV questionnaire assesses beliefs through "propositional, declarative statements about sales behavior." This surface-format matching — text-based instruction followed immediately by text-propositional belief measurement — could produce better ADAPTS scores in the control condition independent of any genuine learning difference. The paper acknowledges "this alternative explanation cannot be ruled out from the present data" (p. lxv).

However, this concern is listed as the sixth of eight limitations, presented as roughly co-equal with sample size and attrition issues. From a measurement validity perspective in training research, construct-irrelevant variance from format matching is not a minor limitation — it is a potential fundamental confound in the primary outcome comparison. If any proportion of the control group's ADAPTS advantage reflects surface familiarity with propositional description of selling behaviors rather than genuine belief change, the game vs. control ADAPTS comparison becomes uninterpretable as an assessment of training effectiveness.

**Why it matters**: The aggregate ADAPTS null — which the paper interprets as "a warning against undifferentiated GBL deployment" rather than "the game is inferior" — relies on the comparison being a valid apples-to-apples assessment of adaptive selling belief change. The format–measurement alignment concern puts this interpretation at risk.

**Suggestion**: Elevate this limitation in the Discussion. Consider adding a supplementary analysis: does the ADAPTS gain in the control group correlate with time-on-task in the text module more than time-in-game correlates with ADAPTS gain? Such a pattern would support the format-match interpretation. At minimum, discuss more explicitly in §6.7 why practical recommendations based on the ADAPTS comparison must be treated as conditional on this alternative explanation being ruled out.

**Severity**: Major

### W4: Control Condition Adaptive Selling Content Is Insufficiently Described
**Problem**: The control condition is described as "a conventional digital module covering equivalent adaptive selling content through propositional text slides and scenario-based practice in Phase 2" (paraphrased from §4.2). This description does not specify: what content was covered (which specific adaptive selling frameworks or competencies), how much content there was, whether Phase 2 scenarios were scored or unscored, and whether the control included feedback mechanisms. Without this information, readers cannot evaluate whether the game and control delivered genuinely equivalent content, which is the essential prerequisite for interpreting the comparison.

**Why it matters**: If the control module was more content-complete or more structured in its practice opportunities, the null ADAPTS finding could reflect content advantage rather than format advantage. If the control was shorter and less structured, the paper's claim that it was a "non-minimal-effort baseline" requires more documentation.

**Suggestion**: Provide a content outline or summary of the control module in §4.2 or as a supplementary appendix. At minimum, report the number of content screens, the structure of Phase 2 scenarios in the control (number, type, feedback format), and whether the content was produced by the same developers as the game.

**Severity**: Minor

## Detailed Comments

### Introduction
The motivation is clear and well-grounded in the practical relevance of sales training effectiveness. The gap identified (prior knowledge as moderator) is genuine and understudied in sales training specifically.

### Literature Review / Theoretical Framework
The sales competency literature (Moncrief & Marshall, 2005; Cummins et al., 2013; Barling & Beattie, 1983) is appropriately cited to situate ADAPTS-SV as the outcome construct. However, the HRD training transfer literature (Blume et al., 2010 is cited) could be more extensively engaged to contextualise why immediate post-test belief measures are a limitation inherent to the training evaluation paradigm, not just to this study specifically.

### Results / Findings
The completer demographic table (§4.4 or §5.1) should confirm the industry breakdown of game and control participants separately — if the education sector majority is distributed differently across conditions, this could confound the comparison at baseline.

### Discussion
§6.7 (Implications for B2B Sales Training Design) is the paper's most practically oriented section and the one that a training practitioner would be most likely to act on. It would benefit from one paragraph addressing the organisational barriers to implementing expertise-routing: who administers the pre-ADAPTS, at what point in the training workflow, and whether the recommendation to direct novices to "direct instruction first" requires a separate track or a gating mechanism. These practical questions are answerable at the level of recommendations even without additional data.

### Conclusion
The Conclusion's "precision prescription" framing is memorable but should be qualified: it is a precision prescription for the sample and outcome construct measured here, not for B2B sales training broadly.

## Questions for Authors

1. What was the occupational breakdown of the game and control groups separately (not combined)? Were the education-sector participants distributed comparably across conditions, and did their ADAPTS baseline scores differ from the industry participants?

2. Were participants self-employed professionals, employed salespeople, or students in professional development programs? This distinction matters for interpreting the pre-ADAPTS scores as baseline adaptive selling expertise — a professional salesperson with 5 years of experience and a pre-ADAPTS score below 46.7% is a very different case from an educator enrolling in a sales skills development course.

3. The transfer intention measure (Tho & Trang, 2015) is described as capturing "post-training intention rather than demonstrated on-the-job transfer." Was any effort made to follow up participants on actual training transfer? Even a brief 4-week follow-up for a subset would be valuable; is this possible?
