### Summary Assessment
This paper makes a genuine and timely contribution to the serious games literature for professional training. The theoretical reanalysis of the LM-GM framework — identifying mechanics transparency as an implicit and untested assumption, and Bloom-level prerequisite dependency as a structural gap — is the most interesting thing in the paper and deserves publication. The expertise reversal pattern observed is consistent with a well-established body of literature (Kalyuga et al., 2003; Hernández and Moreno, 2019; Tay et al., 2022) and is here tested at the individual-participant level under controlled conditions rather than derived from aggregate cross-study comparison, which is a meaningful methodological step forward.

The paper is mature in its engagement with the literature and generally accurate in its citations. My primary concerns are: (1) the game is not described with enough design detail for readers to evaluate the LM-GM alignment claim or for practitioners to transfer the intervention; (2) there are some notable GBL literature gaps in the review; (3) the interpretation of Hernández and Moreno's (2019) moderator (gaming experience vs. domain expertise) requires careful verification; and (4) the mechanics transparency coefficient concept, while compelling, would benefit from engagement with adjacent constructs in the instructional design literature (e.g., Mayer's coherence principle, Paas & van Merriënboer's efficiency framework) that partially overlap with what is being proposed.

Overall, I recommend Minor Revision. The paper is ready for publication with targeted improvements to the game description and literature engagement; the theoretical contribution is sound and publishable.

## Strengths

### S1: Genuine and Well-Grounded LM-GM Framework Extension
The paper's identification of mechanics transparency as a necessary condition (not merely a desirable feature) for LM-GM alignment to function in practice is a theoretically rigorous contribution. The argument is well-grounded: Arnab et al. (2015) themselves acknowledge that low-level mechanics "have little pedagogical value in isolation" and only serve learning when subordinated to higher-level goals — which requires that the player has already decoded and automated them. The present paper makes this temporal, sequential cost explicit and formalises it as a design criterion. This is an additive, not contradictory, extension to the framework — exactly the kind of theoretical work that moves a design framework from descriptive to prescriptive utility.

### S2: The Expertise Reversal Interpretation Is Well-Contextualised in the Existing Literature
The discussion in §6.4 correctly contextualises the expertise-conditional ADAPTS pattern within the expertise reversal literature (Kalyuga et al., 2003) and connects it to the structural parallel in Hernández and Moreno's (2019) organisational GBL review. The paper appropriately notes that those authors characterise the moderator as gaming experience rather than domain expertise — an important distinction — and argues that the structural pattern is equivalent. This is honest and nuanced handling of a potentially ambiguous source.

### S3: The Mediation Finding Adds Genuine Mechanistic Clarity to the DGBL Literature
Within the serious games literature, the question of how games produce learning outcomes (through what psychological mechanisms) is far less well studied than whether they work. The finding that SE fully mediates the ENG→ADAPTS pathway — and the further observation that this mediation was detectable only in the game condition (where SE–ADAPTS coupling was significantly tighter) — is a meaningful mechanistic contribution. This aligns with Tay et al.'s (2022) observation that engagement alone does not produce learning outcomes without an intervening confidence-building mechanism, and is here tested in a controlled comparison rather than derived from correlational survey data.

### S4: Appropriate Engagement with the Measurement Timing Problem
The discussion in §6.8 (Limitation 7) of the structural timing disadvantage of immediate post-test comparison for GBL conditions is sophisticated and field-relevant. The argument — that games operate through schema-building, confidence priming, and motivational carry-forward mechanisms that compound post-training — is consistent with Wouters et al.'s (2013) meta-analytic finding that serious game advantages are larger for retention than immediate learning. This is not just a limitation acknowledgement; it is a methodological critique that should influence how the field designs GBL comparative trials.

## Weaknesses

### W1: Intervention Description Is Insufficient for Replication and Alignment Verification
**Problem**: The game intervention is described at the level of structural features (card mechanics, token economy, two-phase structure, branching scenarios, Pavlovian interaction feedback) in §4.2, but the specific content taught — the actual adaptive selling concepts embedded in the cards, scenarios, and feedback — is not described. Similarly, the mapping of game mechanics to learning mechanics (the LM-GM alignment that is the paper's theoretical centrepiece) is asserted but not documented. A reader cannot verify whether the alignment is structurally correct without seeing which mechanic serves which learning function.

**Why it matters**: This is not just a transparency concern — it directly affects the paper's theoretical contribution. If the LM-GM alignment table is not provided, the claim that "the present design followed this principle" (p. lxix) is unverifiable. For practitioners attempting to apply the expertise-routing recommendation, they need to know what game design features triggered the novice overload, not just that "card mechanics" did.

**Suggestion**: Add a supplementary table mapping each of the 5-6 key game mechanics to: (a) their intended learning mechanic (per Arnab et al., 2015 taxonomy), (b) their Bloom level, and (c) the relevant sales competency target. This table would take approximately one page and would substantially strengthen both the replication value and the theoretical contribution. Alternatively, include this mapping in §4.2 as a Table 4.X.

**Severity**: Major

### W2: Several Key GBL Literature Sources Are Missing or Under-Engaged
**Problem**: The literature review, while solid, has gaps that a domain reviewer would expect to see addressed:

(a) **Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (CTML)** — Mayer (2009) is cited once in §7 (Conclusion) but is not engaged in the literature review or theoretical framework, despite being the foundational framework for understanding how game interface elements produce extraneous cognitive load. The coherence principle (eliminate extraneous material) and signalling principle (cue relevant structure) are directly relevant to the mechanics transparency argument and would strengthen §3 and §6.2 considerably.

(b) **Leppink et al. (2013)** — The paper recommends this instrument for future studies in §6.9, acknowledging it provides validated intrinsic/extraneous/germane subscales — yet the paper's own CL decomposition uses an ad hoc subscale with α=.31 that approximates the germane construct. The paper should either defend the choice of its own CL instrument over Leppink et al. or acknowledge more directly in the literature review why the Leppink instrument was not used.

(c) **Adaptive instruction and dynamic difficulty adjustment literature** — The paper recommends "dynamic difficulty adjustment" as a future design intervention (§6.7, §6.9) without citing any existing DDA literature in GBL (e.g., Serrano-Laguna et al., 2012; Zohaib, 2018) that would ground this recommendation in existing design knowledge.

**Why it matters**: These gaps suggest the literature review is thorough on the empirical GBL effectiveness side but thinner on the instructional design and cognitive science foundations that underpin the paper's primary theoretical contribution.

**Suggestion**: Add a brief engagement with CTML in §2 or §3, particularly the coherence and signalling principles. Cite Leppink et al. (2013) in the measures section and explain the choice of the alternative CL instrument. Add one or two DDA citations when discussing dynamic difficulty adjustment as a design recommendation.

**Severity**: Minor

### W3: The Game Description Underspecifies the Control Condition
**Problem**: The control condition ("digital module with non-linear navigation, content skipping, and a scenario-based Phase 2 component") is described in less detail than the game condition throughout §4.2. For an experimental comparison, the control condition's design features matter: how interactive was it, what content was presented, what was the Phase 2 scenario structure? The paper notes (§6.8, Limitation 9) that the control was not a "minimal-effort baseline" and includes evidence of selective engagement (39% with sub-10-second inter-slide gaps). But without more detailed description of the control module's design, readers cannot fully evaluate the experimental contrast.

**Why it matters**: The comparative claim (game vs. conventional digital instruction) depends on what "conventional" means in this context. A control with its own Phase 2 scenario component reduces the distinctiveness of the game manipulation, which should be acknowledged more prominently in the design rationale.

**Suggestion**: Add a supplementary description of the control module's structure (number of slides/screens, content sequence, nature of Phase 2 scenario, answer feedback mechanism). A design comparison table (Game vs. Control on key design dimensions) in §4.2 would make the contrast explicit and would preempt reviewer concerns about whether the "game" advantage is mainly being tested against a genuinely different instructional approach.

**Severity**: Minor

### W4: Bloom-Level Prerequisite Dependency Concept Would Benefit From Engagement With Adjacent Frameworks
**Problem**: The Bloom-level prerequisite dependency argument (§7, lxx–lxxi) is intuitive and well-supported by the Arnab et al. (2015) framework's own mechanics taxonomy. However, adjacent frameworks in instructional design that address the same sequential prerequisite problem — most notably Merrill's (2002) First Principles of Instruction (especially the prerequisite and progression principles) and Gagné's (1985) conditions of learning (prerequisite hierarchies) — are not cited. The BPD concept would be stronger if positioned within this established instructional design tradition rather than derived solely from the LM-GM context.

**Why it matters**: Positioning BPD within the broader instructional design literature would both strengthen the theoretical claim and make it legible to readers who know instructional theory but not GBL-specific frameworks.

**Suggestion**: Add a brief paragraph in §6.5 or §7 connecting the Bloom-level prerequisite dependency to Gagné's prerequisite conditions or Merrill's progression principle, noting that the present study provides an empirical illustration of a sequencing failure predicted by these foundational frameworks.

**Severity**: Minor

## Detailed Comments

### Title & Abstract
The title is effective and directly poses the research question. The abstract accurately represents the paper's contribution. I note the abstract describes the cluster analysis as "exploratory" — this is appropriate and consistent with §6.8; the paper should use this designation equally consistently in the Results and Discussion sections.

### Literature Review / Theoretical Framework
The multi-theory integration (CLT + Bandura's SE theory + engagement + LM-GM) is handled competently. The CLT discussion in §2.2 is appropriately grounded in Sweller et al. (2011) and Paas et al. (2003). The LM-GM section in §3.3 is the theoretical framework chapter's strongest component. One point: the paper cites Arnab et al.'s (2015) Figure 3 (Bloom taxonomy mapping) as the basis for the BPD argument, but this specific figure maps mechanics to Bloom levels at the framework level, not for the specific game used in this study. The paper should clarify whether it applies Arnab et al.'s general framework-level mapping to the specific mechanics of its own game design, and if so, whether this mapping was performed a priori (at the design stage) or post-hoc (as a retrospective interpretive analysis).

### Methodology / Research Design
The procedure section (§4.4) describes the intervention delivery adequately. The absence of a fidelity check (e.g., was the content of both conditions verified to cover equivalent adaptive selling concepts?) is a limitation not explicitly noted in §6.8.

### Results / Findings
The cluster analysis results (§5.6) are clearly presented. The cluster profiles (C1 Novice, C2 Moderate, C3 Advanced) are theoretically interpretable. The ARI=1.000 stability result is reassuring.

### Discussion
§6.4 is the paper's most domain-insightful section. The identification of the CLT mechanism for the expertise reversal is theoretically precise and consistent with the GBL expertise reversal literature. The paper correctly distinguishes "gaming experience" (Hernández and Moreno's framing) from "domain expertise" (this paper's framing), although a direct test of gaming experience as a moderator would have been valuable.

### Conclusion
The three-part conclusion (Theoretically / Practically / Methodologically) provides a clear and well-structured payoff. The mechanics transparency coefficient framing is memorable and publishable.

## Questions for Authors

1. Was the LM-GM alignment of the specific game mechanics (token economy, card selection, cascading information, Pavlovian feedback) mapped a priori at the design stage, or was this mapping performed retrospectively as part of the theoretical analysis? If retrospective, this should be acknowledged — post-hoc framework mapping can introduce interpretive flexibility.

2. Why was the Leppink et al. (2013) validated CL instrument not used, given that its subscales directly correspond to the intrinsic/extraneous/germane distinction that the paper's CL decomposition attempts to measure? What instrument was used for CL measurement, and what is its source and validation history?

3. The paper reports that gaming experience was not measured. Given the expertise reversal literature's characterisation of the moderator as gaming experience (Hernández & Moreno, 2019), does the absence of a gaming experience measure constitute an alternative explanation that cannot be ruled out — i.e., that the C1 novice cluster's disadvantage reflects low gaming experience rather than low domain expertise?

## Minor Issues

### Language / Grammar
- p. lxviii: "the game significantly tightened SE–ADAPTS coupling" — technically, the Fisher z comparison is a between-condition test, not a causal claim about the game tightening coupling. Suggest "was associated with significantly tighter SE–ADAPTS coupling compared to the control condition."

### Citation Format
- Hernández and Moreno (2019): the citation in §6.4 paraphrases their finding accurately, but the direct quote format used in the next sentence (from Tay et al.) is not applied here. Consistency of citation practice would improve readability.

### Figures and Tables
- A supplementary LM-GM alignment table would be the single most impactful addition to this paper from a domain expert's perspective.
- Consider adding a visual representation (timeline or flow diagram) of the two-phase game structure in §4.2 to make the intervention design concrete.
